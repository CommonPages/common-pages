Decreased ventilation of the Southern Ocean in glacial time is implicated in most explanations of lower glacial atmospheric CO2. Today, the deep (>2000 m) ocean south of the Polar Front is rapidly ventilated from below, with the interaction of deep currents with topography driving high mixing rates well up into the water column. We show from a buoyancy budget that mixing rates are high in all the deepwaters of the Southern Ocean. Between the surface andâˆ¼2000m depth, water is upwelled by a residual meridional overturning that is directly linked to buoyancy fluxes through the ocean surface. Combined with the rapid deep mixing, this upwelling serves to return deep water to the surface on a short time scale.
