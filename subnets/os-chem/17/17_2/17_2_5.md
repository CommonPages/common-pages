name: The Presence of a Catalyst

Relatively dilute aqueous solutions of hydrogen peroxide, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, are commonly used as topical antiseptics. Hydrogen peroxide decomposes to yield water and oxygen gas according to the equation:

Under typical conditions, this decomposition occurs very slowly. When dilute H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>(aq) is poured onto an open wound, however, the reaction occurs rapidly and the solution foams because of the vigorous production of oxygen gas. This dramatic difference is caused by the presence of substances within the wound’s exposed tissues that accelerate the decomposition process. Substances that function to increase the rate of a reaction are called  **catalysts**, a topic treated in greater detail later in this chapter.
