name: Phosphorus Oxygen Compounds

Phosphorus forms two common oxides, phosphorus(III) oxide (or tetraphosphorus hexaoxide), P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub>, and phosphorus(V) oxide (or tetraphosphorus decaoxide), P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub>, both shown in {'Figure_18_8_P4O6P4O10 Figure 18.8.P4O6P4O10}. Phosphorus(III) oxide is a white crystalline solid with a garlic-like odor. Its vapor is very poisonous. It oxidizes slowly in air and inflames when heated to 70 °C, forming P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub>. Phosphorus(III) oxide dissolves slowly in cold water to form phosphorous acid, H<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>3</sub>.

Phosphorus(V) oxide, P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub>, is a white powder that is prepared by burning phosphorus in excess oxygen. Its enthalpy of formation is very high (−2984 kJ), and it is quite stable and a very poor oxidizing agent. Because of its great affinity for water, phosphorus(V) oxide is an excellent drying agent for gases and solvents, and for removing water from many compounds.
