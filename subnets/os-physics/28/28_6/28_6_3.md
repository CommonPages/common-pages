It is even more interesting to investigate what happens to kinetic energy when the velocity of an object approaches the speed of light. We know that <math xmlns:m="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:md="http://cnx.rice.edu/mdml" xmlns="http://cnx.rice.edu/cnxml"><semantics><mrow><mrow><mi>γ</mi></mrow><mrow/></mrow><annotation encoding="StarMath 5.0"> size 12&#123;γ&#125; &#123;&#125;</annotation></semantics></math> becomes infinite as <math xmlns:m="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:md="http://cnx.rice.edu/mdml" xmlns="http://cnx.rice.edu/cnxml"><semantics><mrow><mrow><mi>v</mi></mrow><mrow/></mrow><annotation encoding="StarMath 5.0"> size 12&#123;v&#125; &#123;&#125;</annotation></semantics></math> approaches <math xmlns:m="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:md="http://cnx.rice.edu/mdml" xmlns="http://cnx.rice.edu/cnxml"><semantics><mrow><mrow><mi>c</mi></mrow><mrow/></mrow><annotation encoding="StarMath 5.0"> size 12&#123;c&#125; &#123;&#125;</annotation></semantics></math>, so that KE<sub>rel</sub> also becomes infinite as the velocity approaches the speed of light. (See {'Figure_29_6_3a Figure 29.6.3a}.) An infinite amount of work (and, hence, an infinite amount of energy input) is required to accelerate a mass to the speed of light.

So the speed of light is the ultimate speed limit for any particle having mass. All of this is consistent with the fact that velocities less than <math xmlns:m="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:md="http://cnx.rice.edu/mdml" xmlns="http://cnx.rice.edu/cnxml"><semantics><mrow><mrow><mi>c</mi></mrow><mrow/></mrow><annotation encoding="StarMath 5.0"> size 12&#123;c&#125; &#123;&#125;</annotation></semantics></math> always add to less than <math xmlns:m="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:md="http://cnx.rice.edu/mdml" xmlns="http://cnx.rice.edu/cnxml"><semantics><mrow><mrow><mi>c</mi></mrow><mrow/></mrow><annotation encoding="StarMath 5.0"> size 12&#123;c&#125; &#123;&#125;</annotation></semantics></math>. Both the relativistic form for kinetic energy and the ultimate speed limit being <math xmlns:m="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:md="http://cnx.rice.edu/mdml" xmlns="http://cnx.rice.edu/cnxml"><semantics><mrow><mrow><mi>c</mi></mrow><mrow/></mrow><annotation encoding="StarMath 5.0"> size 12&#123;c&#125; &#123;&#125;</annotation></semantics></math> have been confirmed in detail in numerous experiments. No matter how much energy is put into accelerating a mass, its velocity can only approach—not reach—the speed of light.
