name: Rotation Angle

When objects rotate about some axis—for example, when the CD (compact disc) in {'Figure_7_1_1aa Figure 7.1.1aa} rotates about its center—each point in the object follows a circular arc. Consider a line from the center of the CD to its edge. Each  **pit** used to record sound along this line moves through the same angle in the same amount of time. The rotation angle is the amount of rotation and is analogous to linear distance. The  **arc length**<math xmlns:q="http://cnx.rice.edu/qml/1.0" xmlns:m="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:md="http://cnx.rice.edu/mdml" xmlns="http://cnx.rice.edu/cnxml"><semantics><mrow><mrow><mspace width="0.25em"/><mtext>Δ</mtext><mi fontstyle="italic">s</mi></mrow><mrow/></mrow><annotation encoding="StarMath 5.0"> size 12&#123;Δs&#125; &#123;&#125;</annotation></semantics></math> is the distance traveled along a circular path as shown in {'Figure_7_1_1ab Figure 7.1.1ab} Note that <math xmlns:q="http://cnx.rice.edu/qml/1.0" xmlns:m="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:md="http://cnx.rice.edu/mdml" xmlns="http://cnx.rice.edu/cnxml"><semantics><mrow><mrow><mi>r</mi></mrow><mrow/></mrow><annotation encoding="StarMath 5.0"> size 12&#123;r&#125; &#123;&#125;</annotation></semantics></math> is the  **radius of curvature** of the circular path.

We know that for one complete revolution, the arc length is the circumference of a circle of radius <math xmlns:q="http://cnx.rice.edu/qml/1.0" xmlns:m="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:md="http://cnx.rice.edu/mdml" xmlns="http://cnx.rice.edu/cnxml"><semantics><mrow><mrow><mi>r</mi></mrow><mrow/></mrow><annotation encoding="StarMath 5.0"> size 12&#123;r&#125; &#123;&#125;</annotation></semantics></math>. The circumference of a circle is <math xmlns:q="http://cnx.rice.edu/qml/1.0" xmlns:m="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:md="http://cnx.rice.edu/mdml" xmlns="http://cnx.rice.edu/cnxml"><semantics><mrow><mrow><mn>2π</mn><mi>r</mi></mrow><mrow/></mrow><annotation encoding="StarMath 5.0"> size 12&#123;2πr&#125; &#123;&#125;</annotation></semantics></math>.
