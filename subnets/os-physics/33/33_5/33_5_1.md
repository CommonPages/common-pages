name: Conception of Quarks

Quarks were first proposed independently by American physicists Murray Gell-Mann and George Zweig in 1963. Their quaint name was taken by Gell-Mann from a James Joyce novel—Gell-Mann was also largely responsible for the concept and name of strangeness. (Whimsical names are common in particle physics, reflecting the personalities of modern physicists.) Originally, three quark types—or  **flavors**—were proposed to account for the then-known mesons and baryons. These quark flavors are named  **up** ( *u*),  **down** ( *d*), and  **strange** ( *s*). All quarks have half-integral spin and are thus fermions. All mesons have integral spin while all baryons have half-integral spin. Therefore, mesons should be made up of an even number of quarks while baryons need to be made up of an odd number of quarks. {'Figure_34_5_1 Figure 34.5.1} shows the quark substructure of the proton, neutron, and two pions.
